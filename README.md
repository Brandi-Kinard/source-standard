# The Source Standard

### Where did this actually come from?

Give it any claim, article, URL, or topic.
It traces the chain backward until it finds the earliest
traceable origin in the accessible public record.

Not the most popular source.
Not the most authoritative-seeming source.
The earliest one we can actually verify.

---

## What It Does

1. Traces the citation chain backward through accessible
   public records — Internet Archive, .gov, .edu,
   digitized newspapers, academic databases,
   legal filings, congressional records
2. Shows you where the chain starts — and where it breaks
3. Identifies the gap between the earliest traceable
   source and the version that became widely accepted
4. Flags where the official record goes silent —
   because an absence of sources is itself a signal
5. Lets you build and export a source library

---

## What It Doesn't Do (Yet)

The public record is not the complete record.

Institutional sources — .gov, .edu, textbooks,
official archives — are the foundation of v1 because
they're accessible, indexed, and verifiable via
existing APIs.

But the public record reflects who controlled
the archive. Records were selectively preserved.
Histories were deliberately excluded. The most
important primary sources for many communities
exist nowhere in any indexed database.

v1 traces what's traceable.
The gap between what's traceable and what's true
is the next problem.
